Tattoo-Friendly Baths,
Mapped

The most common question a tattooed visitor asks about Japanese bathing has no single answer, because there isn't one law behind it, only a patchwork of house rules. Here is what we could verify, facility by facility, with the date we checked it.

By Takayama Sauna, Editor-in-Chief · Sauna producer, supervisor of award-winning facilities · 5 min read · July 2026

Every tattooed visitor who plans a trip to a Japanese bathhouse asks some version of the same question: will they let me in? Search for an answer online and you'll find lists that contradict each other, blog posts that read like they were written once in 2018 and never touched, and at least one still recommending a bathhouse that closed in 2019. The confusion isn't really about tattoos. It's that nobody is publishing a rule all of Japan actually follows.

That's because there isn't one nationwide rule. The Japan Tourism Agency and the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare have held a consistent position since 2016: refusing someone entry solely because of a tattoo is inappropriate, and a tattoo does not in itself pose a hygiene risk. How that plays out differs by facility type, though. Neighborhood bathhouses (sento) fall under the Public Bathhouses Act and prefectural ordinances, which restrict an operator's right to turn someone away without proper cause, part of why a blanket nationwide ban has never existed. Hotel and ryokan baths, large day-use complexes, and standalone saunas sit outside that framework, setting their own house rules, most inherited from a decades-old custom of using tattoo checks to keep organized-crime members out of communal bathing.

That is why this piece exists as a map rather than a verdict. We checked each facility's official website or published notices rather than trusting aggregator lists that keep recycling each other's errors, publishing only what we could confirm ourselves, with the date we checked it.

The rules, decoded

Among the official policies we reviewed, three recurring approaches emerge.

Fully permitted. No covering or prior approval required: tattoos are simply a non-issue.

Cover-conditional. The facility will admit you if the tattoo is fully concealed with a facility-approved tattoo-covering patch, sized and capped differently house to house. A tattoo too large to cover within a given facility's limit is treated the same as an uncovered one.

Fully prohibited, cover or not. These facilities reject tattoos outright, though the wording differs: some rule out cover patches explicitly, others simply bar entry without addressing patches at all. The distinction matters, as Spa World shows below.

The trap in that third category is assuming a famous name signals tolerance. It doesn't. Sauna Shikiji in Shizuoka, widely known among international sauna enthusiasts, states plainly that tattoos are not admitted, cover patches included (saunashikiji.jp, checked 17 July 2026). Spa World in Osaka, a thermal theme park visitors may assume is permissive given its size, is stricter: its official usage guide (spaworld.co.jp/info/tebiki.pdf, checked 17 July 2026) states that anyone with irezumi, "including tattoo stickers," is refused entry outright, no cover-patch exception published anywhere on the site. That's a different basis than Shikiji, which names seals as a non-option; Spa World never offers seals to begin with. Both land in "fully prohibited," but the fine print differs.

There's a second failure mode: information rot. Jakotsuyu, a once-beloved Asakusa sento long cited as a reliably tattoo-friendly landmark, closed in May 2019. Older guides that remain online still list it. A published tattoo policy is only as good as the date attached to it, the entire premise of the list below.

Fully OK, no conditions

  • Daikoku-yu Onsen (Oshiage, Tokyo): its FAQ states tattooed guests are welcome, no qualification attached (daikokuyu.com/services.html, checked 17 July 2026).
  • Koganeyu (Kinshicho, Tokyo): the same direct answer on its official FAQ (koganeyu.com/posts/QandA, checked 17 July 2026). Also the featured house in our earlier report, The Sento Renaissance.
  • Nuka to Yuge (Kyotango, Kyoto Prefecture; enzyme bath with private-reservation saunas): its FAQ answers the question directly, "Yes, you're welcome to use the facility" (nuka-yuge.com/qanda, checked 17 July 2026). Featured in our piece The Bath With No Water.

The 2026 headline: a major hotel group drops the rule

A January 2020 industry-press interview (DANRO) with the head of the Onyoku Shinko Kyokai (Bathing Promotion Association) reported that only three of its roughly 180 member facilities had no tattoo restriction at all, a snapshot of the old default's grip; the figure is six years old and may no longer hold. Compare that to March 1, 2026, when Mitsui Fudosan Hotel Management lifted tattoo-related bathing restrictions across the 24 domestic properties with communal baths among its Celestine, Mitsui Garden, and sequence brands, citing rising international demand (mitsuifudosan-hotelmanagement.com/bath-info, checked 17 July 2026). That's a notable development, not yet proof of an industry-wide trend: one operator's properties, not a market-wide sample.

Cover-conditional

Tokyo Toyosu Manyo Club (tokyo-toyosu.manyo.co.jp/tatoo-info, checked 17 July 2026) and Saya no Yudokoro in Itabashi (sayanoyudokoro.co.jp, checked 17 July 2026) both specify 11cm x 20cm, two patches. Solaniwa Onsen Osaka Bay Tower (solaniwa.com/en-us/news/tattoos, checked 17 July 2026) requires full coverage without stating a size. SAUNA METSÄ OIMACHI in Tokyo, opened March 2026, uses its own 9.5cm x 14.0cm patch, up to two (ryusenjinoyu.com/saunametsaoimachi, checked 17 July 2026). Four facilities, three specs. Call each facility to confirm its current patch rules.

Prohibited outright

Sauna Shikiji (saunashikiji.jp, checked 17 July 2026) and Spa World (spaworld.co.jp/info/tebiki.pdf, checked 17 July 2026), as above. Add Shumeikan, a hot-spring lodge at Ubako Onsen in Hakone: a notice posted 1 March 2026 states admission is refused "regardless of number, size, or visibility" (shumeikan.biz, checked 17 July 2026). Its operator, Tenzan Toji-kyo, also runs Tenzan, which by its own account allows tattoos for solo bathers only, a reminder that policy is set facility by facility. See The Onsen That Refuses to Be a Spa.

There's a gap here, more honest to show than hide. Kosugiyu in Koenji and Funaoka Onsen in Kyoto are widely described online as tattoo-friendly, consistently across independent sources, but neither publishes a policy on its own site, so we're not listing them as confirmed. One plausible reading: older neighborhood sento, run for decades on the assumption tattoos were never worth a rule, often never wrote one at all. Treat that absence as unknown, not permission. Call ahead.

The fallback, and where policy goes from here

If a facility's policy is unclear, or its patch size won't hide your tattoo, there's a workaround the Japan Tourism Agency has recommended since 2016: book a private bath. Kashikiriburo (reservable private baths) and a ryokan room with its own bath remove the shared-space question, but check first: some properties apply a tattoo policy site-wide, private bath included, and a room bath's own terms are worth confirming too.

None of this is fixed. A facility open today can tighten its rule tomorrow, and, as Jakotsuyu shows, a beloved one can simply close. Confirm directly before you go, and treat any list, including this one, as dated rather than permanent.

The facilities we could individually confirm sit in Tokyo and Osaka, plus one Shizuoka warning case and one new entry in rural Kyotango, Kyoto Prefecture; we still have no confirmed policy inside Kyoto City itself. It isn't the full country, and patch terms especially deserve a facility-by-facility call rather than a rule of thumb.
