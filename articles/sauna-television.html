Why Do Japanese Sauna Rooms
Have Televisions?

It's the first culture shock most European visitors have in a Japanese sauna: a screen bolted above the stove. The honest history behind it is thinner than the standard story, and our search of the platform Sauna Ikitai found television-equipped sauna rooms outnumbered roughly three to one — a platform snapshot, not a verdict on the whole country.

By Takayama Sauna, Editor-in-Chief · Sauna producer, supervisor of award-winning facilities · 6 min read · July 2026

I host European guests in Japanese saunas most months of the year, and one question comes up with enough regularity that I could set a stopwatch to it. A guest looks up from the bench expecting the dim hush of a Finnish löyly room and finds a flat-screen television mounted above the stove in a 90°C room, tuned to a variety show or a sumo bout, volume on. Finnish sauna etiquette treats quiet conversation and outright silence as roughly interchangeable, on the shared assumption that nobody forces the room's attention onto anything but the heat — a screen has no place in that grammar. So the reaction is rarely offense. It's closer to disbelief, read not as a design choice but as a mistake, as though Japan imported a Finnish institution and got the room wrong on the way in.

That reading doesn't survive a look at where the television actually came from, and it survives even less contact with the newest facility data on how many Japanese sauna rooms still have one. Both are worth walking through, because the honest answer turns out to be less tidy, and more interesting, than the explanations that circulate online.

Where the TV came from

Public sauna bathing in Japan traces to a wave of facilities that opened in Tokyo in the late 1950s and early 1960s, with the practice gaining broader national attention around the 1964 Tokyo Olympics, when the story is usually compressed into "Finnish athletes brought saunas to Japan." The more precise, documented starting point sits a few years earlier: a Japanese Olympic marksman encountered the saunas at the 1956 Melbourne Games and, shortly after returning, installed a dry sauna at Tokyo Onsen, an entertainment bath complex that had operated in Ginza since 1951. The 1964 Olympics are still the moment most Japanese sauna historians treat as the trigger for the format's first commercial spread; Tokyo Onsen is where it actually entered the country.

The television looks like it belongs to a later chapter. Through the 1980s and into the 1990s, Japan went through a second, larger wave of bathing facilities: health lands and super sento (large-format public bathhouses run more like day-resorts than neighborhood baths), where televisions were a familiar fixture throughout many of these complexes — changing areas and dining halls included — as a matter of course. The sauna room, on this reading, simply inherited a habit the rest of the building already had.

I want to be direct about a limit here. I could not find a facility record, trade publication, or manufacturer archive documenting who installed the first television in a Japanese sauna room, or why. What exists instead is a consistent, secondhand explanation, repeated for years across enthusiast blogs and forums: that a screen gives bathers something to fix their attention on through a long, hot sit, and that sumo and baseball broadcasts in particular turned the sauna room into a small, recurring social occasion for regulars. This is industry folklore, and I'm treating it that way here.

What the numbers actually show

The "got the room wrong" reading is the part worth revisiting. On 17 July 2026, our editorial team ran a search of Sauna Ikitai, Japan's largest sauna-review platform, filtering separately for listings with and without a television in the sauna room. The result: 3,130 listings show a sauna room with a TV; 11,513 show one without — television-equipped listings are outnumbered roughly three to one on the platform.

Three caveats belong right next to that figure. Sauna Ikitai is a user-submitted database, compiled by enthusiasts rather than audited by a government body or trade association, so the count reflects entries on the platform rather than an audit of every sauna room in the country. A single facility can also operate more than one sauna room, so the two searches can overlap — a property with both a TV room and a screen-free room shows up in both totals, so these aren't a clean, mutually exclusive split. And users motivated enough to log a facility's TV status may skew toward the newer, quieter end of the market. Read the ratio as a strong signal, not a settled fact.

What this snapshot shows is today's distribution — we have no historical Sauna Ikitai data to compare it against, so "fading" is a bigger claim than this dataset can carry. My reading, offered as a reading rather than a finding, is that television-equipped rooms are concentrated in an aging slice of the market rather than spread evenly across it. The health lands and super sento that built the habit in the 1980s and 90s are, as far as I can tell, largely still in business, and many of them still operate today, televisions included. What looks different is what got built more recently: Shibuya SAUNAS, produced by manga artist Katsuki Tanaka and opened in December 2022, and The Sauna in Nojiriko, a reference point for Japan's post-2019 boom, both describe rooms built around low light and quiet rather than a screen. Whether that pattern holds up once you cross-tabulate opening year against facility type — Showa-era health land versus post-2019 boutique sauna — is exactly the question a platform-wide count can't answer, and it's what our paid report is built to test.

How to read a TV sauna

I'd push back on the instinct to treat a television-equipped sauna as a lesser attempt at a Finnish one. It functions as an extension of the Showa-era communal living room: a shared screen in a shared hot room, built for regulars who came back often enough that the broadcast became part of the rhythm of the visit. That's a legitimate social design, not a mistranslation.

If I'm honest about my own leanings as a producer, I design for silence more often than not, because the projects I take on tend to sell an experience closer to Finland's than to a health land's. But the two answer different design questions. A market where a visitor can deliberately choose either one is healthier than a market where every room defaults to the same script.

For a visitor, the practical move is simple: Sauna Ikitai lets you filter listings for sauna rooms without a television, and it's worth using before you book. If you'd rather sit inside the older tradition, look for the health-land and super-sento buildings that carried the format through the decades, sumo broadcast included.

This piece covers the shape of the split; the fuller breakdown — where each tradition concentrates by opening year and facility type, and the methodology behind the Sauna Ikitai numbers — is reserved for our quarterly report, The Japanese Sauna Market, Decoded. Which room you choose also changes the itinerary that follows it, a distinction we build into every custom trip through our Bespoke service, whether the goal is the newer generation's quiet or the noisier, communal version that came before it.
